200

The Kushana, a central Asian people, built a powerful empire in 1st century CE. They spread Buddhism to central and eastern Asia, while their cultural influence shaped Indian art. Northern India saw the rise of kingdoms and flourishing of Hinduism and Buddhism. The Satavahana kingdom constructed impressive cave temples. Southern India witnessed the establishment of warring kingdoms, engaged in overseas trade with the Roman Empire and Southeast Asia, fostering a thriving network of towns and a strong money economy.

500

The Gupta empire emerges as a powerful Indian dynasty, ruling over northern India and exerting influence in central regions through alliances. However, their power wanes due to invasions by the Huns and the rise of independent subordinate kingdoms. Despite this, the Gupta era is hailed as a brilliant period in ancient Indian history, marked by advancements in science, art, literature, and the development of the decimal system of numbers. In South India, the kingdoms of the Pandyas, Chola, and Pallavas thrive, focusing on maritime trade. While trade with the declining Roman Empire diminishes, South East Asia becomes a significant trading partner, catering to the economic demands of south China.

750

Northern India: Harsha briefly unites the region before large regional kingdoms emerge, including Gurjara-Pratihara, Pala, and Chalukya, setting the stage for power struggles. Southern India: Pallavas dominate, engaged in continuous conflicts with Chalukyas and facing a renewed threat from the Pandyas. Buddhism declines, with Hinduism gaining prominence as the dominant religion in the sub-continent.

980

In northern India, the Gurjara-Pratihara and Pala dynasties decline, giving rise to subordinate princes known as Rajputs. Meanwhile, in the Deccan region, the Rashtrakutas replace the Chalukyas, expanding their empire through military conquests until their overthrow in 973 by the Later Chalukyas. In southern India, the Chola kingdom emerges as the dominant power, overcoming the Pallava and Pandya kingdoms before facing defeat and territorial loss to the Rashtrakutas. This period also witnesses the rise of Hinduism, which contributes to the decline of Buddhism in the sub-continent as popular Hindu cults offer similar spiritual experiences and social equality to low-born Indians.

1210

India witnesses a significant historical shift as Muslim states establish their presence in the northern region. Mahmud of Ghazna conducts raids and conquers the Punjab, marking the beginning of Muslim invasions into the sub-continent. While the Rajput princes initially resist, internal conflicts weaken their resistance, allowing the establishment of various Muslim sultanates, notably the powerful Sultanate of Delhi. In the Deccan, the Chalukya kingdom disintegrates into several large states. In contrast, the Chola kingdom in south India experiences a resurgence, expanding its territories within India and overseas. However, by the early 13th century, the Chola dominance faces challenges from other emerging powers, particularly the resurgent Pandya dynasty.

1450

The Delhi Sultanate's expansion and subsequent disintegration over the past two and a half centuries have shaped India's history. The sack of Delhi by Timur in 1398 further fragmented the sultanate, leading to the emergence of numerous small states in northern India. Despite Muslim rulers holding power in various regions, religious tolerance prevails, with Hinduism remaining the predominant faith among the population. Muslim rulers employ Hindus in senior government positions, fostering a fusion of Muslim and Hindu artistic and architectural elements. In the south, the Bahami Sultanate and the Vijayanagara kingdom, representing Islam and Hinduism respectively, engage in frequent conflicts as they vie for supremacy.

1650

The Mughal Empire, established by Babur in the early 16th century, has become a dominant power in the Indian sub-continent. Under the reign of Akbar the Great, the empire reaches its peak in terms of cultural, economic, and internal stability, fostering tolerance between Muslims and Hindus and allowing for a unified ruling class. In South India, the Bahmani Sultanate and Vijayanagara Kingdom have fragmented into multiple states. Sikhism emerges in northwest India, promoting peace, tolerance, and rejection of religious hierarchies and the caste system. Meanwhile, European trading bases, predominantly belonging to the Portuguese, Dutch, French, and English East India Companies, dot the coastal regions by the mid-17th century.

1790

The Mughal Empire expanded throughout the 17th century, covering most of the Indian sub-continent by 1700. However, internal divisions, rebellions, and invasions led to its decline in the 18th century, allowing powerful governors to establish independent states and giving rise to new powers like the Maratha Confederacy. The Sikh kingdoms emerged in the northwest as a response to Mughal persecution. Concurrently, European trading companies, notably the British East India Company and the French East India Company, became entangled in the region's conflicts, vying for commercial interests. Ultimately, British dominance prevailed, fueled by their superior navy, leading to the acquisition of significant territories in northern India by the British East India Company.

1870

After Ranjit Singh's death, the Sikh empire descended into chaos, allowing the British East India Company to take control. In 1857, a rebellion erupted, threatening British rule. It was eventually suppressed, leading to direct British governance. The British expanded into Burma and attempted but failed to control Afghanistan. Industrialization began, including the development of a railway system in India.

1914

Under British rule, India was governed by a Viceroy appointed by the British government, overseeing a civil service that administered the sub-continent directly and indirectly through native princes. The British confronted Russia in Central Asia during the "Great Game" and engaged in the 2nd Afghan War. India entered the industrial age with the construction of an extensive railway network, road-building, and the establishment of postal and telegraph systems. While some sectors, like textiles and mining, modernized, the majority of the population continued to live in rural villages, maintaining traditional ways of life.

2005

South Asia's history has been marked by a history of hostilities among its states, culminating in the 1971 war where India supported the independence of Bangladesh. Indira Gandhi, India's prime minister, became more authoritarian after the war and declared a State of Emergency in 1975, but democracy was reinstated in 1977. Since the 1990s, India has undergone significant economic liberalization, leading to rapid growth and the emergence of a thriving middle class.
